## Direct Air Capture (DAC) and Carbon Sequestration Technologies: A Comprehensive Comparison

### Overview

Direct air capture (DAC) and carbon sequestration technologies are crucial for mitigating climate change by reducing atmospheric CO2 levels. ### Scalability

Scalability is essential for widespread adoption of DAC and carbon sequestration technologies. Currently, several technologies are being developed to achieve large-scale deployment:

* **Post-combustion capture**: This method involves capturing CO2 from power plant flue gas, which can be scaled up using existing industrial infrastructure.
* **Oxygen-electrolysis**: This process uses electrolysis to split water into oxygen and hydrogen, with the CO2 being captured at the anode. This technology has shown promise for large-scale deployment but requires significant investment in infrastructure.
* **Direct air capture (DAC)**: DAC technologies directly capture CO2 from the air, which can be scaled up using industrial processes.

#### DAC Technologies:

* **Orca**: Developed by Climeworks, Orca is a DAC system that uses a proprietary membrane to capture CO2. It has been demonstrated at scale in various pilot projects.
* **Carbon Engineering**: Carbon Engineering's DAC technology involves reacting CO2 with water and oxygen to form carbon dioxide carbonate. This process can be scaled up using industrial processes.

* **Capital costs**: Building large-scale capture facilities requires significant investment in infrastructure.

#### Cost-Effective DAC Technologies:

* **Liquid Air Power**: This technology uses liquid air as a byproduct of the electrolysis process. The resulting CO2 is captured and used for various industrial applications, reducing energy requirements and capital costs.
* **Carbon Engineering's Direct Air Capture**: Carbon Engineering's DAC technology has been shown to be cost-effective in pilot projects, with a competitive price point compared to traditional capture methods.
